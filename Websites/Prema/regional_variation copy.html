Looking at Energy Poverty Throughout the EU

These two maps show important metrics associated with energy poverty in the EU. The first map illustrates the percentage of the people at risk of poverty in each EU member state in 2022. The second map depicts the overburden of housing costs by highlighting regions where households or individuals spend a significant amount of their income on housing, which may restrict their capacity to pay for necessities like energy. Together, these maps shed light on how housing affordability and economic vulnerability interact in the EU. Energy poverty issues are likely to be exacerbated in areas with high housing cost burdens and poverty risk. Residents of these areas may experience both fundamental economic hardship and high energy expenses, which can result in inadequate energy access and subpar living conditions. The combination of these two measures highlights the necessity of focused policy initiatives to address the two main causes of energy poverty in the EU: housing insecurity and economic inequality.

Examination by Region in the European Union

The bar chart depicts the average combined poverty and housing overburden for each region of the EU in 2022. The average values are shown by the bars, each of which represents a different region (e.g., Western Europe, Eastern Europe, etc.). By emphasizing areas with greater or lower average costs, this figure offers a brief comparison of how poverty and housing cost issues influence various regions. Different colors are used to aid differentiate between different regions.

The box-plot shows how housing overburden and combined poverty are distributed in each region in 2022. The median is shown by a line inside each box, which represents the interquartile range (IQR). Any outliers are identified separately, and the “whiskers” display the data’s distribution. Finding areas with more consistent or widely differing experiences with poverty and housing expenses is made easier with the help of this graphic, which offers insights into how these challenges vary and spread throughout each region.

Examination by Household Type and Region

These bar graphs show the average poverty rate by household type in several regions of the European Union. Western Europe, Eastern Europe, Northern Europe, Southern Europe, and the EU as a whole are all represented by distinct graphs. To illustrate how the household makeup affects the probability of living in poverty, the poverty rates are divided into general household groups. The following categories are utilized in the graphs:

  • Single-parent homes: two-adult households with dependent children, and multi-adult households with children are all considered households with dependents.

  • Households without Dependents: This group includes households with just adults living there, such as single-person households or households with no dependent children.

  • Other Household Types: Any remaining household arrangements that don’t neatly fall into one of the other two groups are included in this category.

    We may see patterns within each region by displaying the data in this manner, including whether households with dependent children are more likely to be poor than those without. Additionally, by comparing different locations, the graphs make it easier to see which areas have higher or lower rates of poverty according to the type of household.
